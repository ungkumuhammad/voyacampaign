# Copywriting Principles — Proof Over Adjectives

> The rule every Voya caption, hook, carousel line and ad must pass before it ships.
> Strategic/reference version of this principle: `Voya/Marketing/Copywriting_Principles.md`.
> Campaign context: read `Social-Media-Plan` and `Campaign-Master-Calendar.md` first.

---

## The rule

**Stop describing Voya with adjectives. Show the proof and let the viewer conclude.**

*Premium. High quality. Best in class. Trusted. Seamless.* These don't sell — they signal you have
no real proof and reached for a superlative instead. Every unverifiable claim gets scrolled past.

People don't believe bigger words. They believe sharper proof. **Big words get ignored; proof gets
remembered.**

---

## The example to keep in your head

A normal car ad says *"the car is silent."* David Ogilvy's 1958 Rolls-Royce ad said:

> **"At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock."**

One line outsold a thousand adjectives — because the reader *feels* the silence instead of being
told to. (Ogilvy, 1958, *Ogilvy on Advertising*.)

---

## The three moves (use these when writing any caption)

| Don't write | Write instead |
|---|---|
| "It's fast." | The time saved — *"Save two hours."* |
| "It's trusted." | **Who** trusts it — the name, the count, the badge. |
| "It's durable." | What it **survived** — the test, the proof. |

Always: **replace the conclusion you want with the fact that forces it.**

## ⚠️ Teaser-phase rule (Jun → Aug 2026)

During the **Whisper / Curiosity / Anticipation** teaser, the product features —
**Trust, Float & Match, Smart Search** — are deliberately **held back** for the September reveal
(`Social-Media-Plan`, `Campaign-Master-Calendar.md`). So the proof-led *product claims* below are
for **reveal and launch copy**, not the mystery phase.

But the discipline still governs every teaser line: in the teaser, the "proof" is the **feeling and
the specific image**, not the marketplace mechanic. *"Stop opening 15 tabs"* (an existing campaign
motif) is concrete and relatable; *"trip planning made premium"* is dead on arrival. Specifics beat
superlatives even when you're saying nothing about the product.

## Before you post — 5-second checklist

1. **Adjective audit.** Every adjective: swap for a fact / number / name, or cut it.
2. **Skeptic test.** Would the line survive someone who assumes you're exaggerating?
3. **Show don't claim.** Are you handing over the detail, or just the conclusion? Hand over the detail.
4. **Fabrication check.** Every number sourced or a marked placeholder. No invented stats — ever.
5. **The clock test.** Is there one sharp detail a viewer would repeat to a friend? If not, find
   Voya's "electric clock."
